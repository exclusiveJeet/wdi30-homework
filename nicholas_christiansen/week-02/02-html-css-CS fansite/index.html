Who is Claude?

The American mathematician and computer scientist who conceived and laid the foundations for information theory. His theories laid the groundwork for the electronic communications networks that now lace the earth.

1916

Born April 30, Petoskey, Michigan, United States

  • Graduated from Gaylord High School in 1932.
  • Loved all things mechanical and electrical things.
  • His best subjects were science and mathematics.
  • Built model planes, a radio-controlled model boat and a half mile long telegraph system.
  • Childhood hero was Thomas Edison, who he later learned was a distant cousin.

1936

Graduated from U. Mich., with degrees in electrical engineering and mathematics

  • Went to MIT, for graduate studies, where he worked under computer pioneer Vannevar Bush and the “Differential Analyzer”, considered one of the most advanced analog computers in the world at the time.
  • His master's thesis “A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits”, establishes an important connection between Boolean algebra and telephone switching electrical circuits. i.e. "True/False" and 1 or 0
  • In 1940 he received his Ph.D. degree from MIT for his thesis "An Algebra for Theoretical Genetics"

1941

Joined Bell Telephone Laboratories as a research mathematician

  • Worked on war-related matters, including cryptography. His work was used to buil secret communication systems which Roosevelt and Churchill communicated during the war.
  • Unknown to those around him, he was also working on the theory behind information and communications.

1948

Establish Information Theory with ‘A Mathematical Theory of Communication’

  • Information theory studies the quantification, storage, and communication of information.
  • It was originally proposed to find fundamental limits on signal processing and communication operations such as data compression.
  • Shannon developed information entropy as a measure of the uncertainty in a message while essentially inventing the field of information theory.
  • The concept of entropy was an important feature of Shannon's theory, which he demonstrated to be equivalent to a shortage in the information content (a degree of uncertainty) in a message.

1956

Started teaching at MIT

  • Shannon joined the MIT faculty to work in the Research Laboratory of Electronics (RLE). He continued to serve on the MIT faculty until 1978.

2001

Shannon died at the age of 85, on February 24, 2001.
