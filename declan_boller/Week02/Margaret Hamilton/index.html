1936

born August 17, 1936, Paoli, Indiana, U.S.A

1958

Graduated from Earlham College in Richmond studied mathematics and philosophy

Late 1950's

Accepted a job at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) while her husband attended Harvard Law School.

Early 1960's

joined MIT’s Lincoln Laboratory, where she was involved in the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE) project, the first U.S. air defense system.

Late 1960's

Moved on to work at MIT’s Instrumentation Laboratory, which provided aeronautical technology for the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA).

1969

Developed the software for the guidance and control systems of the in-flight command and lunar modules of the Apollo missions.

Mid 1970

Hamilton left MIT

1976

Cofounded the company Higher Order Software

Mid 1986

Established Hamilton Technologies

2003

Hamilton is awarded NASA’s Exceptional Space Act Award

2016

Awarded Presidential Medal of Freedom by Barrack Obama

Fast Facts

Hamilton was involved in the first ever version of the U.S. Air Defence System. Her code allowed the systems to detect enemy aircraft.

Hamilton was one of the first computer software programmers; she created the term "software engineer" to describe her work.

She helped write the computer code for the command and lunar modules used on the Apollo missions to the Moon in the late 1960s and early ’70s

Interesting Trivia About Margaret Hamilton

#1

During the weekends she would often take her daughter to the lab to allow her to spend time with her daughter. While her daughter slept on the office floor, Margaret programmed away, creating routines that would ultimately be added to the Apollo's command module computer.

#2

Received highest amount of money ever received by a NASA Exceptional Space Act Award recipient ($37, 000)

#3

Apollo 11 source code was released on GitHub in 2016 - the first full line of the software read,
"SUBMITTED: MARGARET H. HAMILTON DATE: 28 MAR 69 / M.H.HAMILTON, COLOSSUS PROGRAMMING LEADER / APOLLO GUIDANCE AND NAVIGATION."

#4

Her 4 year old daughter pressed a button accidentally, which started a program that would cause the test environment program to crash, NASA denied her request to create a patch because an astronaut wouldn’t ever make that mistake. Five days into the live mission, one of the astronauts did exactly that and erased all the data that would get the astronauts home.
